# Stakeholder Evidence: Perspective Validity & Systemic Implications

## Overall Stakeholder Evidence Quality Assessment

Stakeholder evidence provides irreplaceable insight into how management malpractice manifests across the professional sports ecosystem and how different institutional actors perceive both the problem and potential solutions. The quality and validity of stakeholder evidence depends heavily on the alignment between stakeholder interests and truth-telling incentivespractitioners with reputational stakes in malpractice denial may provide less reliable assessment than those with interests in reform. However, carefully weighted across diverse stakeholder perspectives with explicit attention to bias and incentive structures, stakeholder evidence illuminates institutional dynamics that quantitative data and peer-reviewed research cannot independently capture.

## Stakeholder Perspective Quality Framework

Stakeholder evidence quality varies systematically based on several dimensions: (1) **directness of experience** whether stakeholders directly experience problem consequences (athletes, medical staff) versus observing from institutional distance (league executives, owners); (2) **alignment of interests with truth-telling** whether stakeholder positions benefit from acknowledging malpractice (unions, athletes, reformers) or from denying/minimizing it (agents with reputational stakes, league offices protecting institutional image); (3) **accountability mechanisms** whether stakeholder positions carry professional or reputational consequences for accuracy (journalists, researchers) versus operating behind confidentiality barriers (internal organizational decision-makers); (4) **access to information** whether stakeholders have institutional proximity to actual practices (team medical staff, athlete advisors) or rely on secondhand knowledge (media, academic researchers).

Athletes and medical staff occupy high-validity positions on experience dimension but face constraints on public disclosure: athletes fear career retaliation, team physicians face employment consequences. Their perspectives are most credible regarding what happens in practice, but public availability of this evidence is constrained. Player unions aggregate institutional power and interest-alignmentthey benefit financially and reputationally from addressing malpracticemaking their public positions relatively credible. Journalistic and independent researcher perspectives provide accountability mechanisms (reputation stakes, professional standards) supporting credibility, though they may lack institutional proximity to actual practices.

Conversely, league executives and team owners occupy lower-validity positions where institutional interests may conflict with malpractice acknowledgmenttheir organizations face liability exposure from documented negligence. Their public statements should be weighted more cautiously for bias, though their private knowledge of organizational failures often exceeds public positions. Agents and financial advisors occupy mixed positions: ethical practitioners with high interest-alignment in reform carry greater credibility, while those with reputational stakes in current opaque systems warrant skepticism.

## Systemic Pattern Recognition Across Stakeholders

Despite diverse perspectives, consistent patterns emerge across multiple independent stakeholder groups regarding how malpractice operates: (1) **vulnerable population targeting** malpractice disproportionately affects athletes in their first 3-5 years, when inexperience and desperation for income make them susceptible; (2) **information asymmetry exploitation** managers, financial advisors, and medical personnel possess expertise that athletes cannot easily verify, enabling self-interested decisions; (3) **institutional incentive misalignment** organizational structures (team ownership, agent compensation models) create financial rewards for practices that harm athlete welfare; (4) **silence maintenance through retaliation** athletes who speak publicly about malpractice face career consequences, institutional isolation, and reputational damage.

These patterns emerge independently across athlete testimony, union analysis, journalist investigations, and organizational documentationtheir cross-stakeholder consistency strengthens confidence in their validity despite individual stakeholder biases.

## Integration with Other Evidence Types

Stakeholder evidence functions most powerfully in conjunction with scientific and practitioner evidence: where peer-reviewed research establishes "what happens," stakeholder testimony explains "why it matters" and "how it feels" to affected populations. Where practitioner evidence explains "what works," stakeholder assessment illuminates "what resistance will emerge" and "whose incentives must shift" for reforms to succeed. Stakeholder evidence alone provides insufficient basis for decision-making, but combined with other evidence types, it ensures that interventions address not only statistical problem prevalence but also the lived experiences and systemic incentive structures perpetuating malpractice.

Overall Assessment
Overall, the stakeholder evidence is **credible, well-aligned across multiple independent sources, and strong enough to confidently inform conclusions** about how malpractice affects athletes. It contains biases and gaps, but these were identified, accounted for, and balanced through triangulation.  

The evidence consistently highlights systemic issues tied to health management, financial oversight, and post-career support. While our sample is NFL-heavy and may emphasize more negative voices, the patterns are supported by diverse sources and remain highly relevant.
